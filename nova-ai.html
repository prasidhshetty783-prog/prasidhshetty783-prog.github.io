Case Study — Masters Project

Nova — Redefining Driver & Vehicle

Nova AI is an intelligent IVIS co-pilot designed to bridge the gap between high-tech automation and road safety. By transforming the In-Vehicle Infotainment System into a proactive partner, Nova uses predictive assistance and context-aware interactions to slash cognitive load — from preempting mechanical failures to adaptive rerouting — so that while the car gets smarter, the driver stays focused on the road.

01 Problem Statement

Smart cars, distracted drivers

Driver interaction with IVIS systems increases distraction and cognitive workload. Complex navigation demands sustained attention, while delayed responses to accidents or mechanical failures can be life-threatening. AI assistants can help through predictive assistance, context-aware interaction, automated emergency detection, and adaptive rerouting.

i.

Cognitive Load

Drivers are bombarded with non-essential data, leading to "glance fatigue" — increasing the risk of lane deviation and delayed braking.

ii.

Trust & Transparency

When a car suddenly suggests a new route or flags a mechanical issue without context, it leads to system abandonment or distrust.

iii.

Context Information

"Engine has an issue" shown only as an icon is stressful and hard to interpret. Exactly what is the issue?

02 UX Road Map

A three-phase user-centered design process

The roadmap followed a three-phase UCD process — Empathize (Aug–Sep), Ideation & Prototype (Oct–Nov), and Testing (Nov–Dec).

03 Empathize Phase

Surveys, observation & 15 research articles

Insights were synthesized from user surveys, observational task analysis, and 15 research articles focused on AI trust, cognitive workload, and multimodal ergonomics.

How might an AI-assisted IVIS be designed to improve safety, usability, trust, and human-AI collaboration while minimizing driver distraction?
Primary Research Question

Secondary research — 15 articles, 3 insights

01

Latency & Reliability

Multimodal sensing requires sub-5-second processing to be effective in emergency scenarios.

02

Human-in-the-Loop

Automated actions (like emergency calling) require a confirm/cancel loop to ensure driver agency.

03

Information Density

Audio-first alerts are critical for significant traffic changes to prevent visual "glance fatigue".

User survey — 26 drivers, aged 18+

01

The "Why" Matters

Drivers explicitly need trusted AI guidance that provides simple explanations alongside route suggestions.

02

Multimodal Clarity

There is strong demand for clear, combined audio and visual reroute alerts.

03

Control and Goals

Drivers are motivated by saving time and avoiding stress — wanting fastest, safest, or fairest route options.

04

Key Pain Points

Delayed traffic updates, inaccurate ETAs, intrusive alerts, and abrupt unfamiliar reroutes with no transparent reasoning.

04 Observational Task Analysis

Watching two drivers in motion

An observational task analysis evaluated real-time interactions with existing IVIS and connected smartphones across three contexts.

01

Navigation & Route Management

Users type before a trip but switch entirely to voice while moving. The most dangerous cognitive spike occurs evaluating and changing routes mid-trip — a clear need for low-effort, transparent rerouting.

02

Communication Friction

Voice assistants fail to recognize contact names (especially with emojis), forcing manual scrolling. Vehicle vibrations cause screen misclicks, drastically increasing distraction.

03

Environment & Media Controls

Touchscreen-heavy climate and media controls pull eyes off the road. Drivers strongly prefer tactile steering-wheel buttons or reliable voice over deep menus.

User personas

Research was synthesized into three personas spanning different levels of tech comfort and trust — guiding the core pillars of Automation, Organization & Transparency.

05 Ideation & Prototyping

Designing for "zones of reach"

Research insights were translated into low-fidelity wireframes using a "Zones of Reach" framework, ensuring the most frequent interactions stay physically accessible with minimal distraction.

i.

Persistent Control Panel

Climate, music, and volume anchored as a persistent layer rather than buried in sub-menus.

ii.

Proximity-Based Layout

Highly interactive widgets (navigation) placed on the left for the closest physical reach in high-stress moments.

iii.

Dedicated AI Zone

A consistent area for AI alerts appears from the right, training the driver's eye exactly where to look.

High-fidelity prototype

The hi-fi interface communicates urgency and context in under a second, without fatigue or night glare.

i.

Dark-Mode Default

A dark, high-contrast palette minimizes cabin glare and reduces eye strain across lighting conditions.

ii.

Color-Coded Urgency

Green for standard reroutes, Yellow for predictive mechanical warnings, Red for critical emergencies.

iii.

Multimodal Feedback

Visuals synchronized with voice — the screen highlights exactly what the AI states aloud.

06 User Testing

Wizard of Oz, three scenarios, SUS 85.83

Testing was conducted under controlled driving simulations using the hi-fi prototype. Participants navigated three critical scenarios — a minor accident detection, adaptive rerouting due to traffic, and a mechanical diagnosis. The "Wizard of Oz" method was used (an instructor imitating the AI), followed by think-aloud, a usability questionnaire, and the System Usability Scale.

Key findings

01

Explainability is a Trust-Builder

A brief "why" (e.g. "Accident ahead") significantly reduced frustration compared to generic "rerouting" alerts.

02

Multimodal Redundancy

Voice prompts plus HUD colour overlays improved situational awareness without increasing perceived workload.

03

Human-AI Collaboration

The "Shared Control" model (AI suggests, human confirms) was preferred over full automation in high-stakes events.

07 Conclusion

From passive interface to proactive co-pilot

Nova AI addresses the paradox of modern infotainment by shifting from a passive interface to a proactive co-pilot. By prioritizing transparency and multimodal collaboration, cognitive load was successfully reduced while maintaining the safety and agency of the driver.

Future improvements

i.

On-Road Validation

Move from simulation to real-world on-road testing to capture environmental stressors.

ii.

Advanced Personalization

Integrate real-time models that adapt to individual driver "alert thresholds" and driving styles.

iii.

Eye-Tracking Integration

Incorporate gaze tracking to refine information density based on where drivers look during alerts.
