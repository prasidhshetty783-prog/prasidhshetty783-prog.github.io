Case Study — Masters Project

NoteLoom — AI Notetaker

An AI-supported study platform designed to help college students manage cognitive overload caused by fragmented study tools and fast-paced lectures. Over 10 weeks, my team and I researched, designed, and tested a solution that centralizes note-taking, summarization, and active recall — improving the SUS score from 78.5 to 97.5 across three rounds of iterative testing.

01 Problem Statement

A chaotic ecosystem of notes, slides & AI tools

College students today navigate a chaotic ecosystem of handwritten notes, PDF slides, and various AI tools. This fragmentation leads to high cognitive load and reduced learning efficiency.

i.

Tool Overload

Students juggle screenshots, slides, and separate AI tools (like ChatGPT), leading to scattered information.

ii.

Organization Debt

Students lose significant time manually reorganizing material before they can even begin studying.

iii.

Speed vs. Structure

During fast-paced lectures, capturing words is prioritized over understanding — often resulting in messy, unusable notes.

02 UX Road Map

A three-phase user-centered design process

The roadmap was divided into a three-phase User-Centered Design (UCD) process — Empathize (Aug–Sep), Ideate (Oct–Nov), and Prototyping & Testing (Nov–Dec).

03 Empathize Phase

Interviews, observation & 18 research articles

Insights were gathered through 1-on-1 user interviews, by observing students' note-taking behavior, and by reviewing research articles focused on AI trust, cognitive load, and multimodal learning.

How can an AI-supported study platform reduce cognitive load for college students while maintaining transparency and user control?
Primary Research Question

Secondary research — 18 articles, 4 key insights

01

Multimodal Learning Accelerates Retention

Relying purely on massive blocks of text is inefficient. The design needed to bridge text and visual representations.

02

"Explainable" and Controllable AI

Students are skeptical of "black-box" AI tools. To build trust, NoteLoom had to be a transparent partner — not a Magic 8-Ball.

03

Active Engagement vs. Digital Distraction

Digital tools often become a source of distraction. We needed an environment that kept students actively engaged.

04

Cognitive Load Optimization

Cognitive overload and disorganized study habits directly contribute to burnout. NoteLoom needed to act as an organizational anchor.

1-on-1 interviews — 7 students, aged 18–35

01

Efficiency & Overload

The cognitive cost of reorganizing notes across multiple apps frequently exceeds the perceived learning benefit.

02

The Speed vs. Comprehension Deficit

During fast lectures, students abandon structured note-taking just to keep up — capturing fragmented text that lacks context later.

03

Trust & Transparency

Students want AI to save time, but are highly skeptical of automated academic assistance that doesn't show its work.

04

Stress Levels

Messy, unstructured materials scattered across PDFs, screenshots, and documents directly trigger anxiety.

User personas

Three personas guided the core feature pillars — Automation, Organization & Transparency.

04 Ideation Phase

Crazy 8s — 48 concepts in eight minutes

The Crazy 8s method was used to generate low-fidelity concepts addressing the "How Might We" statements. "AI sketching" was also tried to expand divergent thinking — though human refinement was necessary to add empathy.

hmw i.

Stay Visually Organized

How might we help students stay visually organized across study material?

hmw ii.

Adaptive Learning

How might we create adaptive learning experiences that suit multiple learning styles?

hmw iii.

Transparent AI

How might we design AI tools that explain reasoning transparently?

hmw iv.

Reduce Overload

How might we reduce overload without diminishing comprehension?

The rapid sketches were translated into low-fidelity wireframes defining the core architecture for note creation, AI-driven summaries, and flashcard generation. By prioritizing layout and logic over aesthetics, the AI integration remained discoverable and user flows stayed centralized and efficient.

05 Prototype & Testing

Three rounds. SUS from 78.5 to 97.5.

Mid-fidelity prototypes established a functional skeleton for the first round of usability testing using a within-subject design. Building on those insights, high-fidelity iterations refined the visual hierarchy and human-AI collaboration until the interface was both intuitive and trustworthy.

Round 1 — Mid-fidelity prototype

Validated the structural logic of the dashboard and core note-taking flow. Two critical friction points emerged: input fields lacked visual boundaries and mimicked static text, and high-value AI tools like auto-summaries and flashcards were buried in nested menus.

06 Key Findings

What three rounds of testing taught us

01

Explicit Affordances are Non-Negotiable

Subtle design cues completely failed with our fast-paced user group. Clear, visible boundaries and explicit text nudges are required to drive engagement and prevent task abandonment.

02

Visibility Drives Adoption

Users overwhelmingly relied on top-level features and avoided nested menus. Surfacing high-value AI actions directly next to the editor instantly removed friction.

03

The Automation vs. Control Tension

AI accelerates the busywork — but students need to remain the decision-makers to stay actively engaged with their learning.

"AI sped up layout ideas… but lacked empathy. It sparked ideas, not decisions."
AI-Teaming takeaway — AI for divergence, humans for convergence

07 Conclusion

Balance between automation and agency

Through three rounds of iterative, human-centered design, NoteLoom evolved from a concept into an intuitive, cognitively supportive study platform. By actively listening to user friction and responding with explicit interaction cues and surfaced navigation, NoteLoom transforms a highly fragmented study process into a centralized, efficient workflow.

Ultimately, NoteLoom succeeds because it strikes a delicate balance — utilizing AI to reduce busywork while preserving the transparency and user agency required for true academic comprehension. The success is reflected in the metrics: a jump from a baseline SUS of 78.5 to a final score of 97.5. NoteLoom demonstrates the immense potential of AI-supported tools when they are strictly anchored to diverse human learning styles.
